Chapter 2: Two Readings

The weeks after the Laishan seminar were the best weeks Maren could remember at the Institute. This was the part nobody warned you about: the argument was fun. The corridors buzzed with it. People lingered after meals to sketch diagrams on napkins. A postdoc in Kassar's lab published a preprint on BDNF promoter accessibility that got fifty-seven internal comments in two days, which was a record. Josip started bringing a second thermos of coffee to the analysis lab because Maren kept drinking his.

"You could bring your own," he said.

"I could," Maren agreed, and did not.

The data had done what data does to a community of scientists: it had given them something to disagree about with precision. The vague, permanent low-grade argument about whether to intervene or to restructure, which had defined the Institute for as long as Maren had been there, suddenly had numbers attached. The BDNF trend was real. The question of what to do about it was no longer theoretical. People picked sides not because they were tribal but because the problem had become specific enough to require a position, and a position, once taken, had a weight that was hard to set down.

Davorka's camp wrote a proposal. It was forty-three pages long and it described, in meticulous physiological detail, a pilot community designed around integrated mechanical exertion. Not a gym. Not an exercise program. A settlement where the infrastructure itself required human physical input to function. Water pumps cranked by hand. Grain mills driven by pedal stations. Heating systems that captured the metabolic output of the people they warmed. The document read like an engineering specification cross-bred with a public health manifesto, and it included energy calculations that Maren checked twice because she didn't believe them the first time. They were correct. Tight, but correct.

Kassar's camp wrote a counter-proposal. It was twenty-one pages long and described a pharmacological and genetic intervention protocol targeting the twelve most affected epigenetic pathways. It was elegant, specific, and supported by two years of animal model data. It could be implemented within existing infrastructure. It did not require anyone to move to a new settlement or restructure civilization. It required a syringe and a Tuesday afternoon.

Maren read both documents on a Saturday, sitting on the flat limestone ledge behind the west annex where she sometimes went to think. The stone was warm. A lizard watched her from the crack in the wall with the fixed, patient expression of something that had been there longer than the building. She read Davorka's proposal first, then Kassar's, then Davorka's again. She made notes in the margins of both. She drew a small chart on the back of Kassar's proposal comparing the projected outcomes over three generations. Both curves went up. They went up at different rates and for different reasons, and neither author would have accepted the comparison, but Maren was a biostatistician and comparing curves was what she did, and both curves went up.

The lizard left. She hadn't noticed when.

Davorka kept a small office on the third floor that smelled like pine resin and old paper. The pine resin was from a diffuser she claimed helped her think. The old paper was from the journals she refused to read on screens. She opened them with a bone-handled letter knife she'd had since graduate school, and Maren had never asked about it because Davorka's objects all had stories and the stories all had lessons and sometimes you just wanted to ask a question about methylation.

"Sit," Davorka said when Maren appeared in her doorway on a Monday morning three weeks after the seminar. She was reviewing something on her desk, her reading glasses low on her nose. She did not look up. Davorka had a way of making you feel expected even when you hadn't announced yourself.

Maren sat. The chair was the same hard wooden chair that had been in this office for the entire duration of Maren's career and probably for some time before that. It was not comfortable, but it was honest about that.

"I read your proposal," Maren said.

"Everybody read my proposal. Kassar's team printed copies to annotate in their weekly meeting. I'm told they used three colors of ink." Davorka looked up. "Which is more attention than most of my work receives, so I'm choosing to be flattered."

"The energy calculations hold."

"I know they hold. I had you in mind when I wrote them. The question isn't whether they hold. The question is whether you think the thing they describe is viable."

Maren took a moment. This was Davorka's way. She didn't ask what you thought about the argument. She asked what you thought about the world the argument implied. It was a different question, and Maren had learned, slowly, that it was the more important one.

"It's viable at small scale," Maren said. "A community of maybe two hundred, in the right climate, with enough technical knowledge to build and maintain the mechanical systems. The energy math works if the population is young and healthy and committed. It stops working if people get old, or sick, or don't want to do it."

"People get old everywhere. What happens when they get old in a society that captures energy from appropriate exertion rather than peak output?"

"Then the system scales to them. You wrote that in section twelve. Resistance-adjustable cranking, rehabilitation exercises routed through capture devices, the output mattering less than the act."

"I wrote it. Do you believe it?"

Maren leaned back in the hard chair. Through Davorka's window she could see the eastern courtyard, where someone had left a bicycle leaning against the oak tree. It had been there for at least a week. Nobody had moved it and nobody had claimed it. The Institute was like that; things found resting places and stayed.

"I believe the principle," she said. "I'm less sure about the implementation. People are not principles."

Davorka took off her reading glasses and set them on the desk. "No. They're not. They're also not the animal models that Kassar is so proud of. They're messier than both, and the mess is where the interesting things happen." She paused. "You've also read Yael's proposal."

"I have."

"And?"

"The curves both go up."

Something shifted in Davorka's face. Not quite a smile. More like the expression of someone who has been waiting for a particular sentence and has just heard it. "They do. The question is what happens when you extend them."

"Past three generations?"

"Past twelve."

Maren hadn't run the projections past three generations. Nobody had, because the error bars became absurd beyond that point and publishing speculative long-range models was the kind of thing that got your methods section eviscerated at review. But she understood what Davorka was suggesting. Somewhere in the building a door closed, and she heard footsteps in the corridor, unhurried, receding. Kassar's intervention corrected a specific pathway. It would need to be re-administered, updated as new pathways degraded, because the correction didn't address the underlying cause. Over twelve generations, the interventions would have to grow more complex while the body they were compensating for grew less capable of compensating for itself.

"That's speculative," Maren said.

"Everything past next Tuesday is speculative. The question is which speculation you trust more: the one that requires an expanding tower of interventions, each one managing something the body used to manage on its own, or the one that works the same way it has for three hundred thousand years."

Maren did not answer. Through the window, the bicycle was still leaning against the oak tree.

"I don't trust either of them," she said. "I trust the data, and the data doesn't go past three generations."

Davorka put her glasses back on. "Fair enough. That's the honest answer." She picked up her pen. "It's also the answer of someone who hasn't decided yet, and at some point, Maren, not deciding is itself a decision. But not today."

Tomas proposed the human trials at the next Institute-wide meeting, and the room divided along lines that had been invisible six months earlier and were now visible to anyone paying attention.

It was not a dramatic meeting. There were no raised voices, just the low hum of the ventilation and the scrape of chairs as people settled. Kassar presented the protocol. It was careful work. Twelve subjects, voluntary enrollment, six-month monitoring period, methylation mapping at four checkpoints, full informed consent. The safety profile from the animal models was strong. The institutional review board had given preliminary approval. Everything was in order.

Davorka asked three questions. They were precise, technical questions about off-target methylation effects in tissues adjacent to the BDNF promoter region. Kassar answered them. The answers were honest: the animal models showed some off-target activity, within acceptable ranges, and the monitoring protocol would catch any drift.

"Acceptable to whom?" Davorka said.

"To the review board. To the statistical threshold established in the protocol. To science."

"The review board evaluates six-month outcomes. I'm asking about six-generation outcomes."

"Nobody can evaluate six-generation outcomes. That's the nature of generational research."

"Which is exactly why I'm cautious about interventions whose consequences we can't evaluate on the timescale that matters."

The room murmured. It was the murmur of people who had heard this argument before but were hearing it arrive, for the first time, at a concrete decision point. Theory had become a twelve-person trial with a start date and a budget line. Davorka's objection was no longer philosophical. It was procedural. She was asking the Institute to delay or deny a research proposal on the grounds that it couldn't prove safety across a timeframe that no research proposal could prove safety across. It was a defensible position and an impossible standard, and everyone in the room knew both things simultaneously.

Maren sat next to Josip and watched the room align. It happened in small movements: who turned toward Kassar when she spoke and who turned toward Davorka. Who nodded at which point. Who crossed their arms. These were not conscious signals. They were the body language of people who had been thinking about this for months and whose thoughts had solidified into postures.

Josip whispered, "I count forty-six on the intervention side, thirty-one on the conditions side, and sixteen who haven't committed to a facial expression."

"You counted."

"I'm a microbiologist. I count things that are alive."

The trial was approved, fifty-eight to thirty-four, with one abstention. The abstention was Maren. She had not planned to abstain. She had planned to vote yes, because the science was sound and the protocol was careful and she believed in following evidence where it led. But when the count came around to her, she found that her hand would not go up for either side, and she sat there with her hands in her lap, embarrassed in a way she couldn't have explained to anyone asking.

Tomas didn't mention the abstention at dinner. He made pasta with the herbs from the courtyard garden and talked about the enrollment timeline and which subjects had already expressed interest. He was happy. His work was moving forward. The eighty-seven percent was about to become a human number instead of a mouse number, and this mattered to him in a way that Maren found both admirable and slightly frightening, though she could not have said what the fear was about.

She washed the dishes while he read on the couch. The water was warm. The window was open. She could hear the cicadas, fewer now than in September; it was getting colder and they were quieting, one by one, the way a sound you've been hearing for months disappears so gradually that you only notice the silence.

The email from the Velden settlement arrived on a Thursday, addressed to the entire Institute, and Maren read it before breakfast because it came flagged as urgent and Maren was the kind of person who could not eat in the presence of unread urgent messages.

The Velden settlement was a small community in the foothills of the Julian Alps, about a hundred and sixty kilometers northwest of the Institute. Five years ago, a group of researchers affiliated with Davorka's network had established it as a pilot project: a village of eighty-seven people living under an integrated exertion protocol. Human-powered water systems, mechanical grain processing, communal cranking stations that stored energy in flywheels and gravity batteries for public infrastructure. It was the smallest possible test of Davorka's thesis, run on a shoestring by idealists, and the Institute's formal position on it was one of interested neutrality, which was a polite way of saying that nobody expected it to produce statistically significant results.

The email contained their first five-year health dataset for the children born in the settlement. Fourteen children, aged one to four. The sample was laughably small. Maren would have dismissed it out of hand if the numbers had been ambiguous.

They were not ambiguous.

Every metric was up. Not by a little. BDNF regulatory markers were twenty-five percent above the matched control group from the nearest conventional town. Myokine pathway activation was off the charts; these were the healthiest inflammatory profiles Maren had ever seen in a pediatric cohort. Bone density, cardiovascular markers, immune function, metabolic gene accessibility, all of it pointing in a direction so consistent that if she had seen it in a pharmaceutical trial she would have assumed data fabrication.

She checked the methodology. It was solid. The control matching was good. The statistical corrections were appropriate for the sample size, which meant the error bars were large, which meant the results were preliminary, which meant nothing was proven.

But the direction was unmistakable. Fourteen children raised in a community where every adult performed daily physical labor that powered the infrastructure they depended on, and those fourteen children were, by every measure the Institute tracked, the healthiest cohort in their database.

Maren sat at the kitchen table with her tea going cold and felt the vertigo of holding two possibilities in balance and feeling one of them get heavier.

Tomas came in, saw her face, and stopped.

"What," he said.

She turned the screen toward him.

He read it standing up. He was quiet for a long time. Then he said, "Fourteen subjects."

"I know."

"Five years. One community. No blinding. No randomization."

"I know."

"This wouldn't survive peer review."

"Probably not. But the direction is there, Tomas. Twenty-five percent on BDNF. In one generation. Without touching the genome."

He set the screen down. He looked at her, and for the first time in their shared history of productive disagreement, she could not read his face. It was not the look of someone whose argument had been weakened. It was the look of someone doing math.

"This doesn't invalidate the trial," he said.

"I didn't say it did."

"But you're thinking it."

"I'm thinking that we now have data going in two directions and I need to figure out what that means."

"It means we have two data points and insufficient information to draw conclusions. You know this. You're a biostatistician."

"I'm a biostatistician who just watched fourteen children blow past every health metric in our database because their parents spent five years cranking water pumps." She paused. "I'm not drawing conclusions. I'm having a feeling."

"I don't work with feelings."

"That's not true and we both know it."

He poured himself coffee. He did not sit down. The morning was the kind of October morning where the light is gold and the air has the specific crispness of a season making up its mind, and Maren noticed this because she noticed things, and also because she was trying not to say anything else about fourteen children in the Julian Alps.

"This is going to be a long week," Tomas said.

It was.
