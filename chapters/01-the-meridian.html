Chapter 1: The Meridian

The cohort data from Laishan arrived on a Tuesday, which Maren remembered later because she'd been eating an apple and had to put it down to make room on her desk. It was the third-generation set. She'd been waiting for it since winter.

She opened the files the way she always opened files from Laishan: carefully, with her morning tea still warm, before anyone else got to the analysis lab. Not because the data was secret. Because she liked the quiet of it. The numbers had a quality when you looked at them before anyone told you what to think about them. They were just themselves. Methylation patterns across four hundred and twelve individuals, aged six to eleven, born to parents born to parents who had never performed sustained physical labor of any kind. Three generations of automation. Three generations of data.

Maren had been a biostatistician at the Meridian Institute for nine years. She was thirty-four. She could describe what she did to other people at dinner parties (though the Institute didn't really have dinner parties; it had dinners, and they were all the same party) as "I look at the numbers before anyone argues about them." This was not entirely a joke. The Institute existed to study the biological effects of sedentary living across generations, and the people who worked there had strong opinions about what the data meant. Maren's job was to sit with the data before the opinions arrived.

The Karst plateau in September was still warm enough to leave windows open. The analysis lab was on the second floor of the main building, and from her workstation she could hear the oak grove that bordered the eastern courtyard. Cicadas, mostly. Wind through leaves that were just beginning to think about turning. The campus had been built into the landscape rather than on top of it, which was either good architecture or stubbornness; the hallways followed the contour of the limestone, and there was a particular staircase near the west annex that nobody used because it was steep enough to qualify as exercise. Tomas used it daily, of course.

She pulled up the methylation profiles and began her standard protocol. Gross metrics first: mean methylation age versus chronological age, variance within the cohort, deviation from the reference population. Then the tissue-specific panels. Then the expression markers that correlated with what the Institute cared about most: BDNF regulation, myokine pathway activation, inflammatory baseline, metabolic gene accessibility.

The gross metrics were fine. They were always fine, at the gross level. That was what made this work maddening and also what made it important. The Laishan children were healthy by every standard clinical measure. Growth curves normal. Cognitive benchmarks normal. Immune function within expected ranges. If you ran them through a standard pediatric assessment, you would find nothing wrong.

Maren did not run them through a standard pediatric assessment. She ran them through the Institute's protocol, which looked at the layers underneath the layers, and this is where things got interesting. Or troubling. Or both; she had not yet decided which word she preferred, and nine years of looking at this kind of data had taught her that choosing the word too early was its own kind of error.

The BDNF regulatory markers were lower than the second-generation cohort. Not dramatically. Not clinically significant, if you were a clinician. But the trend was there, and it was in the same direction it had been moving since the first-generation data: down. A slow, generational dimming of the pathway that regulated neuroplasticity. The children's brains were fine today. The question was whether their children's brains would be fine, and their grandchildren's, and where the curve flattened or whether it flattened at all.

She made a note. She ate her apple.

Tomas was in the kitchen when she got home, which meant he'd had a good day in the expression lab. He cooked when his work went well. When it didn't, they ate at the refectory with everyone else, which was perfectly fine food prepared by people who understood nutrition at a molecular level, but Tomas liked to cook and he was better at it than the refectory staff, and he knew it, and Maren found this particular vanity endearing.

"The Laishan data came in," she said, dropping her bag on the chair that existed for the purpose of having bags dropped on it.

"Third gen?" He was chopping something that smelled like garlic and rosemary. Their apartment was small and looked out onto the western slope of the plateau, where the limestone broke through the soil in long pale shelves. In the evenings the light caught them and made the whole hillside look like a page torn from a book.

"Third gen."

"And?"

Maren took a glass from the shelf. "The gross metrics are clean."

"But."

"I didn't say but."

"You have a but face."

She poured water from the filtered jug. "The BDNF trend is continuing. Down again. Same slope as gen one to gen two." She drank. "It's not a crisis. It's just a line going in one direction for three data points."

"Three generational data points."

"Which is not a lot of data points."

"It's the only kind of data point that matters for this question."

This was the thing about living with someone who worked on the same problem from the other end. Tomas did expression therapy; methylation correction, epigenetic reprogramming, the work of convincing silenced genes to speak again. He was part of the team developing targeted interventions for the exact pathways Maren tracked. She counted the decline; he designed the fixes. They had met at the Institute's welcome dinner nine and a half years ago, when she'd asked him what he did and he'd said "I'm trying to put your graphs back to where they should be," and she'd said "who decides where they should be?" and they'd been arguing about it, on and off, ever since.

It was a good argument. She liked it. She liked that he was smart enough to hold his end and honest enough to change his mind when the data shifted, and she liked that he was wrong about some things in ways that were interesting rather than stupid. She did not yet understand that an argument between two people who love each other is a different thing entirely from an argument between two factions, and that one can survive disagreement and the other cannot.

"Petrič wants to do a full presentation on the Laishan set for the Friday seminar," Tomas said, turning back to the stove.

"Of course she does."

"She's going to use it as ammunition."

"Davorka doesn't use ammunition. She uses data."

"She uses data as ammunition. There's a distinction, but it's subtle."

Maren smiled. This was not entirely unfair. Davorka Petrič, who had trained Maren, who had hired her, who had been at the Institute since before Maren was born, had opinions. They were well-supported opinions, carefully documented opinions, but they were opinions nonetheless, and one of them was that the human body did not need to be engineered out of its current predicament so much as placed back into the conditions under which it functioned correctly. Davorka said this the way other people said water was wet. It did not seem to her like a position. It seemed to her like an observation, and the distinction between those two things was the fault line that ran through the Meridian Institute, although nobody had started calling it a fault line yet. For now it was still called a productive tension.

The Friday seminar was held in the main lecture hall, which seated about a hundred and twenty people and usually held about ninety, because the Institute's permanent staff was ninety-three and the visiting researchers fluctuated between five and fifteen and not everyone came to every seminar, although most people came to Davorka's because she was a good speaker and also because arguing with her was a form of entertainment that the plateau's limited social calendar could not easily replace.

Maren sat in the fourth row, where she always sat, next to Josip Brlan, who ran the microbiome lab and who always saved her a seat because she had once, three years ago, helped him debug a statistical model and he had apparently decided this made them allies for life. Josip was the kind of person who organized his loyalties early and maintained them with a stubbornness that was either admirable or exhausting depending on how much of it was aimed at you.

Davorka stood at the front. She was sixty-one, broad-shouldered, with hair that she cut herself every six weeks with what appeared to be the same pair of scissors she'd been using since before Maren was born. She wore the same style of linen shirt every day, rotated through five colors, and could silence a room by raising one eyebrow. She did not believe in slides. She believed in talking.

"The Laishan third-generation data is in," she began. "Maren has processed the initial metrics and I've reviewed her work. I want to talk about what the BDNF regulatory trend means and what it doesn't mean, because half of you will overreact in one direction and the other half will overreact in the other direction, and I'd like to save us all the time."

A laugh from the room. She was right. The Institute divided, broadly and with many exceptions, into two camps. Davorka's camp believed the data showed that sedentary living was producing generational damage at the epigenetic level and that the correct response was to reintegrate physical exertion into the infrastructure of daily life. Tomas's camp, led more formally by Dr. Yael Kassar, believed the data showed the same thing but that the correct response was to intervene at the genetic and pharmaceutical level to repair and optimize the affected pathways. Both camps agreed on the data. Both were looking at the same numbers Maren produced. They disagreed on what to do about it with the kind of certainty that comes from being very smart and very sure that your interpretation is the obvious one.

Maren had noticed, over the years, that she agreed with different camps on different days depending on which numbers she'd been looking at most recently. She had mentioned this to Davorka once, expecting reassurance. Davorka had said, "Good. That means you're still looking at the data instead of your own reflection." It was the nicest thing Davorka had ever said to her, and Maren had been thinking about it ever since.

Davorka walked through the BDNF findings without hurrying. She put the numbers on the board with a marker, not because she had to but because she thought the physical act of writing helped people take data seriously. Maren suspected she was right about this. There was something about watching a hand draw a downward curve that a projected graph didn't quite capture.

"Three generations," Davorka said, drawing the line. "The slope is consistent. It is not steep. Nobody's children are going to wake up tomorrow unable to form new memories. But the pathway is dimming, and the dimming is heritable, and we are now three data points into a trend that shows no sign of leveling off. The question is not whether this is happening. The question is what you propose to do about it."

She looked at the room. Her eyes found Yael Kassar in the second row.

"Yael, I believe you have thoughts."

Kassar stood. She was younger than Davorka by fifteen years, precise in her speech, and genuinely brilliant in ways that Maren found intimidating. "We have a CRISPR-Cas12 variant in trial that targets the BDNF promoter methylation directly. The second-generation expression correction in animal models is running at eighty-seven percent. We're proposing human trials for next year."

"You're proposing to edit the methylation."

"We're proposing to restore it. There's a difference."

"Is there?" Davorka put the marker down. "You're intervening in an epigenetic pathway that's expressing a particular way in response to environmental conditions. You're proposing to change the expression without changing the conditions. I'd call that editing."

"I'd call it medicine."

The room was quiet the way it got quiet when Davorka and Kassar talked directly to each other. Not tense, exactly. Attentive. The way you watch two people play a game you know both of them are good at.

"The pathway is dimming because the bodies aren't being used," Davorka said. "You want to repaint the gauge. I want to fix the engine."

"Your engine fix requires restructuring the entire built environment around mandatory physical labor. My gauge fix requires a single-course gene therapy that a child can receive at age two."

"And when it doesn't catch the other eleven thousand things that sustained physical exertion does for the body? The myokine cascades, the inflammatory regulation, the cardiovascular remodeling, the bone density maintenance, the microbiome signaling, the circadian entrainment, the social bonding from communal effort? You'll develop eleven thousand more therapies?"

"Perhaps."

"That's not science. That's hubris wearing a lab coat."

Kassar smiled. She had heard this before. "And restructuring civilization around crank handles and flywheels is what, exactly?"

"An admission that the system is more complex than our ability to model it."

Maren watched. She had heard this argument, or versions of it, dozens of times. The words changed. The shape didn't. At some point, you had to pour the concrete one way or the other, and she wasn't sure yet which way it should go. On Tuesday mornings, alone with the data, this felt like wisdom. On Friday afternoons, watching the people she respected most in the world disagree about it, it felt like cowardice.

Josip leaned toward her. "Petrič is in good form today," he whispered.

"She's always in good form."

"Kassar's not wrong, though. About the animal models."

"I know."

"You have a face."

"Everyone keeps telling me about my face."

The campus at night was quiet in a way that cities were not. The Karst plateau sat high enough that you could see the lights of the lowland towns to the south, but the Institute itself kept its lighting minimal; something about the founding charter and respecting circadian rhythms, which was the kind of principle that sounded idealistic until you lived there and realized that sleeping well in real darkness was the sort of luxury you didn't want to give back.

Maren and Tomas walked the long way home, through the oak grove and past the weather station that no one had maintained since Dr. Horta retired. The instruments were still there, measuring things and sending the measurements to a database that, as far as Maren knew, nobody checked. She found this oddly comforting. The weather station continued to do its job regardless of whether anyone was paying attention. There was a lesson in that, probably, but she was tired and did not want to draw it.

"Davorka was hard on Kassar today," Tomas said.

"Kassar held her own."

"She always does. That's not the point." He walked with his hands in his pockets, which he did when he was thinking about something he hadn't finished thinking about. "The eighty-seven percent figure is real, Maren. The animal models are solid. We're not guessing anymore."

"You're not guessing about BDNF promoter methylation. That's one pathway."

"It's the pathway everyone's been watching."

"It's the pathway everyone's been measuring. Those aren't the same thing."

He looked at her. It was the look she'd first seen at that welcome dinner, the one that said she'd said something he couldn't easily dismiss and he was going to need a few minutes with it. She liked this look. She also feared it, a little, because there were times when a few minutes turned into a few months and the thing she'd said became part of the architecture of their disagreement, another stone in a wall that she was not sure she was building on purpose.

"Let's not do this tonight," he said.

"I'm not doing anything. You brought up the eighty-seven percent."

"I brought up the eighty-seven percent because I'm proud of my work and I wanted my partner to acknowledge it."

She reached over and took his hand. "I acknowledge it. Eighty-seven percent is remarkable. I mean that."

He laced his fingers through hers. "But."

"No but."

"You have a but face."

"I'm going to start wearing a mask."

They walked. The gravel path crunched under their feet. Somewhere in the oak grove an owl was working through its evening repertoire, which consisted of four notes repeated with a regularity that Maren suspected Josip could have mapped to a statistical distribution if anyone had asked him to. Nobody had asked him to. This was, she thought, also fine.

In their apartment, Tomas fell asleep before she did, which was normal. He slept like a person whose day's work had led to clear results. Maren lay beside him and listened to his breathing and did not think about the Laishan data, specifically, but about the feeling that accompanied it, which was the feeling of looking at a system you could describe but not predict. Like watching water move through limestone. You knew it was going somewhere. You could measure the flow. But the rock was doing things underneath that you couldn't see, dissolving along fractures you hadn't mapped, and the surface measurements were true but incomplete in ways you couldn't quantify, and the honest thing to do with that kind of uncertainty was to sit with it rather than solve it.

She got up, quietly, and went to the kitchen. She poured water and stood by the window. The hillside was dark except for the limestone shelves, which caught enough starlight to be visible. Below the plateau, the lowland towns hummed with electricity she couldn't hear from here. Systems running in the dark, managing things, while the people inside them slept. She wondered whether they'd notice if it stopped.

She did not know yet that the third-generation data from Laishan was the second-to-last dataset she would process at the Meridian Institute. She did not know that within a year, the argument between Davorka and Kassar would stop being the Friday seminar entertainment and start being the thing that broke the community apart. She did not know that she would have to choose.

She finished her water and went back to bed.
