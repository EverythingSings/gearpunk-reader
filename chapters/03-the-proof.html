Chapter 3: The Proof

The human trial began in November, and by January the first checkpoint data was on Maren's desk.

She had asked not to be involved. This was unusual; Maren was involved in everything that generated data at the Institute, because Maren was the person who processed data at the Institute, and the distinction between "being involved" and "doing your job" was not one the Institute typically recognized. But she had gone to the director, an affable man named Pavle who managed the Institute the way some people manage gardens (with patience, periodic weeding, and the understanding that most things grow better if you don't stand over them), and she had said that she did not think she could be objective about the trial data given that her partner was on the research team.

Pavle had looked at her for a long moment and then said, "Maren, nobody at this Institute is objective about anything. That's not actually how science works. But I'll assign Lenka to the first-pass analysis if it makes you feel better."

It did not make her feel better, but it did make her feel more honest, which was close enough.

Lenka Voss was twenty-six, a recent hire from the biostatistics program in Vienna. She approached data with cheerful aggression. She had not yet learned that some numbers had politics attached to them. She processed the first checkpoint and sent the results to the full staff list at 6:47 on a Wednesday morning, which Maren knew because she was already awake, because she had been awake since four, because Tomas had been sleeping badly for weeks and his restlessness had colonized her side of the bed through the invisible mechanisms by which partners transmit anxiety to each other.

The results were good. Not good the way Maren might have hoped they'd be good, which would have been ambiguous enough to require further study and therefore buy everyone more time. Good in the way that closes a question. The BDNF promoter methylation correction in the twelve trial subjects was running at eighty-four percent, which was slightly below the animal model figure of eighty-seven percent but within the expected human translation range. The targeted pathway was responding. The subjects reported improved cognitive clarity, better sleep, measurably better mood regulation. At the four-month mark, every measured metric was trending in the direction Kassar had predicted. And one metric was doing something nobody had predicted: two subjects showed improved inflammatory markers in gut-associated lymphoid tissue, which was not a targeted pathway and had no obvious connection to BDNF methylation. Lenka had flagged it with a question mark and a note that said "artifact?"

Maren read the data at her kitchen table while Tomas slept. The numbers were clean. Lenka's analysis was sound. On the twelve pathways the protocol targeted, the intervention was working. And on at least one pathway nobody had targeted, something else was happening that the model didn't explain. She circled Lenka's question mark and wrote "not artifact" in the margin and did not know what to do with that.

She made herself breakfast. She ate it standing up, looking out the window at the winter plateau. The limestone shelves were dusted with frost, and the oaks were bare, and the sky had the flat gray quality of a January morning that hasn't decided whether to snow. A maintenance truck was crossing the lower courtyard, its engine a low diesel grumble. These were the kinds of sounds you stopped hearing until someone took them away.

The Velden team sent their six-month follow-up two weeks later.

The original dataset had been fourteen children. The follow-up expanded the scope: all eighty-seven residents, full epigenetic panels, plus a battery of cognitive and physical assessments that Davorka's network had been refining for years. The sample was still small. The methodology was still observational, not experimental. These were still the caveats that would make any journal reviewer reach for a red pen.

But the data was getting harder to dismiss.

The adult residents showed epigenetic improvements that tracked with the duration of their participation. People who had been at Velden for the full five years showed the strongest effects. Newer arrivals showed smaller but consistent improvements. The dose-response curve was clean: more years of integrated exertion, more epigenetic normalization. This was the kind of pattern that made observational data start to feel structural rather than coincidental.

Maren ran the adult data through three different analytical frameworks on a Saturday afternoon, her lower back stiff from three hours in the same chair, a half-eaten bowl of lentil soup going cold by her keyboard. Josip sat across the lab pretending to work on his own project and actually reading over her shoulder. He had given up on subtlety about this several weeks ago.

"The myokine panel is extraordinary," he said.

"You're reading my screen."

"I'm reading your screen. The myokine panel is extraordinary."

It was. But it was the sleep architecture data that caught Maren's attention, because it was the one result she hadn't expected. The Velden adults were sleeping differently. Not just better, which you might predict from increased physical activity, but structurally differently: their slow-wave sleep phases were longer, their REM cycling was more regular, and their cortisol patterns had shifted to a curve that Maren had only ever seen in longitudinal studies of pre-industrial populations. Their bodies had reset to a sleep rhythm that belonged to a different era.

Nobody at the Institute had a model for why integrated mechanical exertion would change sleep architecture. There were hypotheses: circadian entrainment from consistent morning physical activity, thermoregulation effects, reduced chronic inflammation allowing deeper recovery cycles. But these were hand-waves, not mechanisms. The sleep data was doing something the intervention data was not: it was producing results that nobody had predicted, in pathways nobody had targeted, for reasons nobody could fully explain.

This was, Maren realized, the twelve-of-twelve-thousand argument made visible. Kassar's trial targeted twelve pathways and improved those twelve. The Velden settlement hadn't targeted anything, and everything was improving, including things that weren't in any model.

She sat back. Josip was still reading.

"Stop looking at my screen."

"I will not."

"The sleep data."

"I see it."

"Nobody predicted that."

"No." He took a sip from his thermos. He'd started bringing a third one. "Nobody predicted the microbiome shifts either, but I didn't want to bring those up until I'd run my own analysis. Their gut flora diversity is remarkable. It looks like the kind of profile you see in populations with high dietary variety and consistent physical load, which is exactly what Velden provides, but the rate of diversification is faster than I'd have expected from five years."

"Faster how?"

"Faster like something is happening at the host-microbe signaling level that we don't have a model for." He set the thermos down. "I'm going to need better data than this to publish anything. But I'm going to spend the next month trying to pretend I didn't see what I just saw."

Kassar published her trial results in March. They were excellent. The paper was meticulous, the peer review was favorable, and the data showed exactly what two years of animal models had predicted: targeted epigenetic methylation correction was feasible, effective, and safe at the six-month mark. The twelve subjects were doing well. The twelve pathways were responding. The intervention worked.

Davorka published the Velden analysis the same month, in a different journal. The paper was more cautious, hedged with the limitations of observational data and small samples. But the results were there: comprehensive, multi-system improvement in a population that had changed nothing except the physical infrastructure of their daily lives. BDNF, myokine, inflammatory, cardiovascular, musculoskeletal, metabolic, sleep, immune, and microbiome markers all trending in the same direction. Including markers that Kassar's trial hadn't measured, because nobody had thought to.

The Institute read both papers in the same week. Maren read them on the same day, sitting at her desk in the analysis lab. She put one down and picked up the other and sat there for a long time looking at the wall.

Both papers were right. That was the problem.

The board meeting happened in April. Maren was not on the board, but Josip was, and he told her about it afterward while they walked the perimeter path that looped around the eastern courtyard and through the old olive grove that nobody had harvested in years because the trees were technically part of a long-term ecological study and also because the olives were terrible.

"They want to expand," Josip said. "Both sides."

"Expand what?"

"Everything. Kassar wants a larger trial. Sixty subjects, five years, multi-pathway protocol. She wants dedicated lab space, dedicated computing resources, dedicated housing for the extended research team." He paused. "Davorka wants a second settlement. Full scale, two hundred people, purpose-built infrastructure, residential commitment. She wants land, construction materials, and a ten-year funding line."

Maren understood. She had been waiting for this without knowing she was waiting. "And the Institute can't fund both."

"The Institute can barely fund one. The endowment covers operations and current research. Either proposal requires external capital, new facilities, and a reallocation of existing staff and resources. Pavle said something about not being able to split the baby." Josip made a face. "I don't think he enjoyed saying it."

"What was the vote?"

"There wasn't one. Pavle tabled it. He asked both sides to present revised proposals at the June meeting with realistic budgets and implementation timelines. He's hoping someone will compromise."

"Will they?"

Josip picked an olive from a low branch, looked at it, and put it back. "Kassar's protocol requires a sterile lab environment, cryogenic storage, a clean room for the CRISPR preparation, and computing infrastructure for the methylation mapping. Davorka's settlement requires farmland, a forge, mechanical workshops, and communal cranking stations. These are not the same building, Maren. You cannot put a clean room in a forge."

They walked. The path curved through the grove and came out near the weather station, which was still measuring, still sending, still unread. It had started to rain, lightly, the kind of rain that isn't quite committed to being rain and might just be the sky making up its mind.

"Josip."

"Yes."

"How did you vote? At the trial meeting."

"I voted yes."

"Why?"

"Because the science was sound and the protocol was careful. The same reason you were going to vote yes before you didn't."

"And now?"

He considered this for a while, which was one of the things Maren liked about him. Other people answered quickly to seem decisive. Josip answered slowly because he was actually thinking. "Now I think the science is still sound and the protocol is still careful. And I also think Velden is producing data that suggests the trial is measuring the wrong things. Both of these are true and I don't know what to do with that."

"Nobody does."

"Petrič does."

"Davorka always knows what to do. That's not the same as being right."

He smiled without conceding anything. "The June meeting is going to be difficult."

They walked back through the rain.

Maren found Tomas in the expression lab that evening. He was cleaning equipment, which he did when he needed to think with his hands. The lab smelled like ethanol, calibration fluid, and the faint ozone tang of the sequencing machines. It was a smell Maren associated with precision and purpose, and she realized, standing in the doorway, that she had always liked it.

"The board tabled the expansion vote," she said.

"I heard." He didn't look up. He was wiping down the micropipette station with the methodical attention he gave to things when he didn't want to talk about what he was actually thinking about.

"Josip says they can't fund both."

"Josip is correct."

She leaned against the doorframe. The fluorescent lights buzzed. The sequencing machines hummed their low electronic hum, steady and continuous, the sound of instruments that never needed to rest. She had never thought about that hum before. It was just the sound of the lab. It occurred to her, standing there, that there was something strange about a room full of machines that never stopped. No winding, no unwinding. No human hand in the cycle. The machines just ran, and the people who used them arranged their lives around the running.

"Your trial is working, Tomas."

He set the cloth down. "Yes."

"And Velden is working."

"Velden is producing observational data from a self-selected community of eighty-seven idealists. That's not the same as working."

"The sleep architecture data."

"Is interesting and unexplained and doesn't prove what Davorka wants it to prove."

"What does she want it to prove?"

He looked at her. The look was not angry. It was tired, which was worse, because Tomas's anger was something she knew how to navigate and his tiredness was something she could only witness. "She wants it to prove that you don't need us. That the body knows better than the people trying to help it. That the twelve pathways we can measure and correct are less important than the twelve thousand we can't." He paused. "And maybe she's right. But 'maybe' is not a research program. 'Maybe' is a philosophy, and I don't build my work on philosophy."

"You build your work on twelve pathways."

"I build my work on the ones I can see."

"What about the ones you can't?"

The sequencing machines hummed. The fluorescent lights buzzed. Somewhere in the building, a door closed.

"That's Davorka's question," he said. "I notice you're asking it now."

She didn't deny it. There was a time when she would have said she was asking it as a biostatistician, as someone whose job was to consider all the data. But standing in the doorway of his lab, watching him clean equipment he had already cleaned, she understood that the question had stopped being professional. She was asking it as a person who had looked at two datasets and felt one of them in her body and the other only in her head.

"I haven't decided anything," she said.

"I know."

"I'm just asking."

"I know." He picked up the cloth again. "But you should know that at some point, asking Davorka's questions is the same as answering them. And once you've answered them, we're on different sides of a building that can't be both things."

She stood in the doorway for a while after that. She could hear the rain on the windows and the hum of the machines and the building settling into its evening sounds.

She went home. Tomas stayed at the lab. She did not wait up for him.
