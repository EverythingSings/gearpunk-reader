Chapter 4: The Ratchet

The June board meeting lasted four hours. Maren heard about it from Josip, who looked, when he found her in the analysis lab afterward, like a man who had been asked to choose between his lungs.

"Kassar gets the east wing," he said. "Sixty-subject expansion, five-year timeline, full computing and lab build-out. The endowment covers it if they reallocate from three existing programs."

Maren waited.

"Davorka gets a letter of support. She can pursue external funding for the settlement expansion independently. The Institute will provide analytical consultation as resources allow." He sat down in the chair across from her desk. "That's not a quote. That's the meaning of the quote, translated from bureaucratic into human."

"So they chose."

"They chose by not choosing. They funded the thing that fits in the existing building and wished the other thing good luck." He rubbed his face. "Pavle tried. He genuinely tried. But you can't build a forge in an east wing that's being converted to a clean room, and the board looked at two proposals and picked the one that didn't require buying land in another country."

The analysis lab was quiet. It was a Saturday, which meant the building was mostly empty, which meant the sounds that were usually buried under the noise of ninety-three people going about their work were suddenly audible: the ventilation system cycling, the tick of the clock on the wall that nobody had replaced with a digital one because this was the kind of institution where analog clocks survived out of gentle neglect, and from somewhere below, the intermittent clang of the maintenance team doing whatever the maintenance team did on weekends.

"How did the vote split?" Maren asked.

"Seven to three. Kassar abstained."

"She abstained?"

"She said it would be inappropriate to vote on her own proposal. Which is technically true and also the most elegant political move I've seen in this building. She gets the funding without having to be seen wanting it."

Maren considered this. It was the kind of thing she might have done, if she were someone who thought in those terms. She was not. She thought in data points and error bars, and what the board had just done was pick the option that was already shaped like the building.

She did not say this to Josip because it was the kind of observation that sounded clever and wasn't helpful.

"What's Davorka going to do?" she said instead.

"What Davorka always does. She has a plan. She had a plan before the meeting. She probably had a plan before the proposal." He paused. "She's already talking to a land trust in Primorska. Coastal site, south-facing, good solar exposure. There's a decommissioned agricultural station with existing water rights."

"How do you know this?"

"Because she told me. Before the meeting. She told me before the meeting, Maren."

This landed differently than Josip had probably intended. Davorka had known. She had walked into that boardroom knowing she would lose the vote and having already planned what to do when she did. The proposal to the board had not been a request. It had been a formality; the last step in a process that Davorka had already completed in her head.

Maren wasn't sure whether this was strategic brilliance or the kind of certainty that made her nervous. Both, probably.

The ratchet, when it came, was not dramatic. It was logistical.

In July, the east wing renovations began. Construction barriers went up in the corridor connecting the east wing to the main building. The noise of drilling and sawing replaced the old quiet of the hallway where Maren had spent nine years walking to the analysis lab. The clean room specifications required sealed air systems, which meant the east wing's ventilation was disconnected from the main building's. The air on either side of the construction barrier began to smell different: the main building still carried the old scent of limestone and floor polish and coffee, while the east wing started to smell like fresh drywall and electrical insulation and something chemical that Maren couldn't identify.

Nobody decided to stop eating together. It just happened. The construction schedule shifted the east wing team's work hours, which shifted their meal times, which meant they started eating at the second seating instead of the first. Within two weeks, the refectory had a first-seating population (the main building) and a second-seating population (the east wing), and the social dynamics of shared meals dissolved without anyone noticing.

Maren noticed. She started eating at both seatings, which meant she ate her main meal with the first group and then sat with a cup of tea during the second, and the east wing team treated this with the careful politeness of people who understood what she was doing and appreciated the gesture without believing it would change anything.

Tomas ate at the second seating. He did not ask her to join him there. She went anyway, most days, and they sat together and talked about things that weren't the expansion and weren't the settlement and weren't the growing list of subjects they had silently agreed to avoid, which was getting long enough that their conversations were developing odd gaps, like a path that keeps routing around obstacles until the detours are longer than the road.

In the third week of July, Maren wrote a proposal. She spent a weekend on it, working at the flat limestone ledge behind the west annex where the lizard used to be. (The lizard was gone. She didn't know if it was the same lizard or if lizards had life cycles she should have understood by now.) The proposal suggested a shared analytical framework: both Kassar's trial and the Velden monitoring would use the same data protocols, the same statistical methods, the same reporting standards. The results could be compared directly. Apples to apples. It was a reasonable document. It was well-structured. It addressed the resource conflicts through scheduling optimization rather than priority ranking. She sent it to Pavle, Kassar, and Davorka on a Monday morning.

Pavle replied within an hour. He said it was thoughtful and he would circulate it.

Nobody else replied. Not that week. Not the week after. The proposal sat in the shared document system the way the bicycle had once sat against the oak tree: present, unclaimed, eventually invisible. Maren checked the access log after two weeks. Kassar's team had opened it three times. Davorka's team had opened it once. Nobody had commented. Nobody had modified their existing protocols.

She did not write another proposal. The lesson was not that compromise was impossible. The lesson was that nobody had time for it. Both sides were building as fast as they could, and a proposal that asked them to slow down and align was a proposal that asked them to do something they no longer wanted to do, which was share the same infrastructure.

The equipment redistribution started in August. It began with a request from Kassar's team for dedicated access to the Institute's primary sequencing platform during the expansion enrollment period. This was reasonable; sixty subjects required a significant sequencing commitment. What it meant in practice was that other labs lost half their scheduled sequencing time. Josip's microbiome work was affected. So was Davorka's team's ongoing analysis of the Velden data.

The request went through a committee. The committee was fair, which did not prevent it from being contentious. Maren sat on the committee because she sat on everything that involved data allocation, and she watched the discussion with the same queasy clarity she'd felt watching the Davorka-Kassar seminars, except that this time the disagreement was not about philosophy. It was about Tuesday afternoons on the sequencer. And somehow this was worse, because you could hold two philosophical positions in productive tension indefinitely, but you could not run two competing protocols on the same machine at the same time.

"This is how it happens," Josip said afterward, walking with her through the courtyard. The bicycle was gone from the oak tree. Someone had finally moved it, or it had been there long enough that the maintenance team claimed it, or whoever had left it had come back for it; there was no way to know. "Not with a speech. With a scheduling conflict. Nobody decides to split. The infrastructure decides for you."

Maren laughed, which surprised her. It wasn't funny. But Josip had said it in the tone of a man making a scientific observation, and the precision of it was amusing in the way that true things sometimes are when you'd rather they weren't.

Davorka's office was being packed when Maren found her there in September, exactly one year after the Laishan data had arrived. Maren had not known Davorka was packing. She had come to ask about the latest Velden numbers, and instead she was standing in a doorway looking at cardboard boxes and the disorder of a space being disassembled by someone who had occupied it for decades.

The pine resin diffuser was on the desk, still running. The letter knife was in a box. The journals were in stacks on the floor, arranged by what looked like a system only Davorka understood.

"I'm not leaving yet," Davorka said, without looking up. She was wrapping something in newspaper. "The land trust finalized last week. We have the site. Construction starts in October. I'm moving the fieldwork office to Koper, which is ninety minutes from the coast site and cheaper than shipping journals twice." She set the wrapped object in a box. "I'll commute to the Institute for the analysis work until the settlement has its own capacity."

"When will that be?"

"Two years. Maybe three. Depends on how fast we can build the analytical engines."

"Analytical engines?"

Davorka looked up. She had the expression she wore when she was about to say something she'd been thinking about for a long time and had been waiting for someone to give her an opening. "We can't depend on the Institute's computing indefinitely. The sequencer allocation proved that. If we're building a community around integrated mechanical exertion, the analytical infrastructure has to be part of it."

"You're talking about mechanical computation."

"I'm talking about building machines that can do population-scale statistical analysis without electricity. Gear-driven calculating engines. Analog, single-purpose, human-powered. Machines that can only do what we wind them to do." She picked up another object. "It's been done. Babbage designed it. The tide-prediction machines worked. Fire-control computers in the second war were entirely mechanical. The question isn't whether it's possible. The question is whether it's practical at the scale we need."

"Is it?"

"I don't know yet. That's one of the things we'll find out." She paused. "I notice you're still standing in the doorway."

"I'm processing."

"You've been processing for a year, Maren. At some point the processing has to produce an output."

This was sharper than Davorka usually was with her, and Maren felt it the way you feel a cut you weren't expecting: the surprise before the sting. She didn't respond immediately. She looked at the boxes, at the stacks of journals, at the diffuser still filling the room with a scent that Maren suddenly realized she associated with being taught something important by someone she trusted.

"Who's going with you?" she asked.

"Twenty-seven people have committed so far. We need fifty for the first phase." She set a box on the floor and straightened. Her knees cracked. "Lenka was going to be one of them. She changed her mind last week. Said she couldn't justify leaving a funded position for something that might not have running water by December." Davorka's jaw tightened. "She's not wrong about the water. But I had her slotted for the analytical work, and now I need someone else, and the list of people who can do population-scale biostatistics without electronic computation is not long."

She was sixty-two now, and the year had put something in her face that hadn't been there before; not age exactly, but the cost of asking people to give up certainty for a principle. "The question you're actually asking is whether I want you to come."

"That's the question I'm actually asking."

"I want you to come. I've wanted you to come since before this started. You know that."

"I know that."

"But I won't ask. You have to decide this without me asking, because if I ask, you'll say yes out of loyalty instead of conviction, and I need people who are there because they believe the premise, not because they love the teacher." She picked up the pine resin diffuser and turned it off. "Go home. Think about it. Don't think about it for too long."

Maren went home. Tomas was cooking, which meant his day had gone well, which meant the enrollment was on track, which meant the thing that was going to take him away from her was proceeding on schedule. She set her bag on the chair. She sat down at the kitchen table. The apartment smelled like garlic and something with rosemary, and she thought about Davorka's office smelling like pine resin and cardboard, and she thought about the refectory with its two seatings, and she thought about the bicycle that was gone from the courtyard, and she realized that she had been making this decision for months without admitting it to herself, the way you stop using a room in your house before you acknowledge that you've stopped.

Tomas turned from the stove. "You're quiet."

"Davorka's packing her office."

He was still for a moment. "When does she leave?"

"October. The site work starts then."

He turned back to the stove. He adjusted the flame. The gas hissed, the steady blue flame doing its invisible work. A pot lid clinked where he'd set it down too close to the edge. He did not ask the next question, which was the question they both knew was there, and Maren did not answer it, and they ate dinner in the sound of forks on ceramic and nothing else.

After dinner she washed the dishes. The water was warm. The window was open. The cicadas were back; a full year had turned since the Laishan data arrived, and the plateau had come around again to September, and the sound was the same.
